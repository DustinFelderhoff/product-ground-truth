# Product Ground Truth

**A framework for PMs who inherit existing products and are expected to make them better.**

Most product managers are handed a product and immediately asked what they want to build next. They start writing roadmaps, pitching features, and chasing the "uniqueness factor" — without ever fully understanding what they already have.

That's where things go wrong.

You can't improve what you don't understand. You can't differentiate in the market if you don't know where you stand. And you can't build on top of a product you haven't taken the time to learn from the ground up — including from the people who actually use it every day.

This framework is my answer to that problem.

---

## The Philosophy

Before a single new feature gets discussed, a PM needs to establish **ground truth** — an honest, evidence-based picture of where the product actually stands today.

That means three things:

**1. Understand your product deeply**
What does it do well? What does it do poorly? Where does it break down for users? What's the gap between what it's supposed to do and what it actually does?

**2. Understand your users honestly**
Not who you think your users are — who they actually are. What are they trying to accomplish? Where do they get stuck? What do they say when nobody's listening? Real user research, not assumptions.

**3. Understand your market clearly**
Where do you sit against competitors? What are they doing better? What are you doing better? What does the feedback data actually say compared to theirs?

Only after you have ground truth on all three should you start talking about what to build next — because now you're building from evidence, not instinct.

---

## Why This Matters

The failure mode I see most often: a PM joins a product, skips the foundation work, ships something "innovative," and six months later wonders why retention didn't move.

The answer is almost always the same — they were solving for novelty instead of solving for the user's actual problem. And they didn't have a baseline to measure against, so they couldn't even tell if they'd improved anything.

Ground truth fixes both problems. It gives you a baseline to measure against. And it forces you to solve real problems instead of interesting ones.

## Who This Is For

This framework is built specifically for PMs who are:

- Joining an existing product with history, tech debt, and a user base already in place
- Being asked to build new features on top of a product they're still learning
- Inheriting a roadmap that was built without a clear baseline
- Trying to make a business case for slowing down and doing the foundational work first

If you're building something brand new from zero, some of this applies — but this framework is really for the harder, more common situation: inheriting something that already exists and being expected to make it meaningfully better.

---

## A Note on Improvement

Ground truth is not an argument against building new things. It's an argument for building the *right* things.

Every improvement this framework recommends starts from a baseline metric — a number, a user sentiment, a competitive gap — so that when you ship something, you can actually tell whether it worked. No more "we shipped X, things feel better" — instead, "we shipped X, retention in this cohort moved from Y to Z."

That's the difference between a PM who ships features and a PM who moves the business.
