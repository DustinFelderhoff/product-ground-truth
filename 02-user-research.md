# 02 — User Research

**Know who actually uses your product, not who you think uses it.**

User research is the step most PMs skip — not because they don't believe in it, but because there's always pressure to move faster. There's a roadmap to fill, a sprint to plan, a stakeholder asking what's coming next.

But skipping user research doesn't make you faster. It makes you wrong faster.

---

## What You're Trying to Answer

- Who are the actual users of this product — not the target persona, the real people?
- What are they trying to accomplish when they use it?
- Where do they struggle, get stuck, or give up?
- What do they say they want versus what their behavior actually shows?
- Who is churning, and why? Who is staying, and why?

---

## The Difference Between Assumptions and Research

Most product teams have user personas. Most of those personas are built from assumptions, not research. They have names and stock photos and bullet points about goals — but they were written in a conference room, not derived from actual user conversations.

Ground truth user research means going back to the source. Talking to real users. Watching them use the product. Reading what they write when they think nobody important is reading it.

The goal is not to confirm what you already believe. The goal is to find out what you got wrong.

---

## How to Run User Research on an Existing Product

### Step 1 — Segment your users first
Before you talk to anyone, understand the different types of users you have. Power users who use it daily. Occasional users who dip in and out. Users who signed up and barely came back. Each segment will tell you something different.

### Step 2 — Run qualitative interviews
Talk to users from each segment. 30 minutes per person. Ask about their goals, their workflows, the last time they used the product and what happened. Don't ask what features they want — ask what they're trying to get done. Listen more than you talk.

### Step 3 — Watch users actually use the product
Session recordings, usability tests, or live observation. What users say they do and what they actually do are often very different. The behavior is the truth.

### Step 4 — Mine your existing feedback sources
App store reviews, NPS responses, support tickets, churn surveys — this is user research that's already been done, you just have to read it. Look for the phrases that come up over and over. Those patterns are signal.

### Step 5 — Separate what users say from what users do
Users will often tell you they want a feature. What they're actually describing is a problem. Your job is to hear the problem underneath the feature request, and then figure out whether that problem is worth solving and how.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Talking only to happy users.** Your best customers will tell you everything is great. You need to talk to churned users, struggling users, and users who almost didn't convert.

**Leading the witness.** "Do you find the dashboard useful?" is not a research question. "Walk me through the last time you logged in — what were you trying to do?" is.

**Skipping research because you used to be a user.** Your experience as a former user is a starting point, not a substitute for talking to current users. The product has changed. The market has changed. Your users' context has changed.
