# 01 — Product Audit

**Understand what you have before you touch anything.**

The product audit is the first thing a PM should do when inheriting an existing product. Not a roadmap review. Not a stakeholder tour. A deep, honest look at the product itself — what it does, how it works, and where it falls short.

This isn't about being critical for its own sake. It's about having an accurate map before you start navigating.

---

## What You're Trying to Answer

- What does this product actually do, end to end?
- What does it do well — genuinely well, with evidence?
- What does it do poorly — honestly, not diplomatically?
- Where is the gap between what it's supposed to do and what it actually does?
- What has already been tried to fix the gaps, and what happened?

---

## How to Run a Product Audit

### Step 1 — Use the product yourself
Not a demo. Not a guided walkthrough. Sit down and use it the way a new user would. Do every core flow. Take notes on every point of friction, confusion, or delight. Your fresh eyes are an asset — use them before you get too familiar.

### Step 2 — Read everything that exists
Support tickets. NPS comments. App store reviews. Sales call notes. Customer success logs. Churn surveys. This is where users tell you the truth, because they're not talking to you directly. Look for patterns, not one-off complaints.

### Step 3 — Talk to the people closest to the product
Support team, customer success, sales — the people who hear from users every day. Ask them: what do users complain about most? What do they love? What questions do they always ask? What makes them cancel?

### Step 4 — Map what the product does vs. what it's supposed to do
Every product has a stated purpose and an actual reality. Document both, side by side. The gap between them is usually where the most important problems live.

### Step 5 — Document with evidence, not opinion
Every strength and weakness you identify should be backed by something — a data point, a user quote, a support ticket volume, a retention number. Opinions are a starting point; evidence is what you'll need when you make the case for where to invest.

---

## What Good Looks Like

A completed product audit should give anyone who reads it — a new stakeholder, a new engineer, a new PM — a clear, honest picture of where the product stands. No spin. No sandbagging. Just an accurate baseline.

If you've done it right, it will be a little uncomfortable to share. That's usually a sign you've been honest enough.
