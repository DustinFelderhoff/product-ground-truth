# 03 — Competitive Landscape

**Know where you stand before you decide where to go.**

Competitive analysis is not about obsessing over what your competitors are building. It's about understanding your position in the market clearly enough to make informed decisions about where to invest and where to differentiate.

You can't know if you're winning or losing without knowing the score.

---

## What You're Trying to Answer

- Who are the real competitors — the ones users actually consider when choosing a product like yours?
- Where are you meaningfully better than them?
- Where are they meaningfully better than you?
- What does the market feedback say about each — reviews, ratings, user sentiment?
- Where is the market moving, and what does that mean for your position?

---

## How to Map Your Competitive Landscape

### Step 1 — Identify your actual competitors
Not just the obvious names. Talk to sales and customer success about who comes up in competitive deals. Read the "alternatives to X" threads on Reddit, G2, Capterra, and Trustpilot. Look at who churned users went to. You'll often find competitors you hadn't considered.

### Step 2 — Use every competitor's product
Sign up for free trials. Go through their onboarding. Run the same core flows you audited in your own product. Take notes on what they do better, what they do worse, and what they do differently. You cannot do this from a marketing page — you have to use the product.

### Step 3 — Read their user feedback
G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, app store reviews, Reddit — the same places you mined feedback about your own product. Look for patterns. What do users consistently praise? What do they consistently complain about? These are your competitor's real strengths and real vulnerabilities.

### Step 4 — Map feature and experience parity
Build a simple matrix: the core jobs to be done in your category, and how each competitor (including you) handles them. Not a checkbox exercise — a nuanced assessment of how well each product actually does each thing.

### Step 5 — Look at positioning, not just features
How do competitors talk about themselves? What do they emphasize? Who do they say they're for? Positioning gaps are often as important as feature gaps — sometimes the opportunity is to be clearer and more specific, not to build something new.

---

## The Right Way to Think About Competitors

Being better than a competitor at everything is not realistic or necessary. Being meaningfully better at the things your best users care about most — that's the goal.

The competitive landscape should help you answer: **where should we double down because we're winning, and where should we close the gap because it's costing us users?**

Both questions matter. And neither can be answered without doing the research first.

---

## What to Watch Over Time

Competitive landscape is not a one-time exercise. Markets move. Competitors ship. New entrants appear. The baseline you establish now becomes a reference point you update periodically — quarterly at minimum, monthly if the market is moving fast.
