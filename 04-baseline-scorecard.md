# 04 — Baseline Scorecard

**One document that tells you exactly where you stand today.**

The baseline scorecard is the output of everything that came before it — the product audit, the user research, the competitive landscape — distilled into a single source of truth.

Its purpose is simple: before you change anything, document what's actually true. So that when you do change something, you can tell whether it worked.

---

## Why a Baseline Matters

Most product teams can tell you what they shipped. Very few can tell you what changed because of it.

That's because they didn't have a baseline. They shipped into the unknown and interpreted the results through optimism. "Things feel better" is not a measurement. A baseline makes measurement possible.

It also makes the case for investment. When you can show a stakeholder exactly where the product stands today — with evidence — and exactly what you're trying to move and by how much, you've turned a conversation about opinion into a conversation about evidence.

---

## What Goes in a Baseline Scorecard

### Product Health Metrics
The numbers that tell you how the product is performing right now.

- **Activation rate** — what % of new users complete the core action that defines value?
- **Retention** — what % of users are still active at 7, 30, 90 days?
- **Churn rate** — what % of paying users cancel per month/quarter?
- **NPS or CSAT** — what do users say about the product overall?
- **Core feature usage** — which features do users actually use, and which are ignored?

### User Sentiment Summary
A distilled view of what users are saying — pulled from research, reviews, and support data.

- Top 3 things users consistently praise
- Top 3 things users consistently complain about
- Most common reason for churn
- Most common request from retained users

### Competitive Position Summary
Where you stand relative to your top 2-3 competitors.

- Areas where you're clearly stronger
- Areas where competitors are clearly stronger
- Areas of rough parity

### Known Gaps
The honest list of things that are broken, missing, or underperforming — with evidence for each.

### Business Metrics
The numbers that connect product performance to business outcomes.

- Revenue or ARR
- Conversion rate (trial to paid, or equivalent)
- Expansion revenue vs. new revenue split
- Customer acquisition cost vs. lifetime value (if available)

---

## How to Use the Scorecard

**Set it as your reference point.** Every initiative you run should point back to a specific metric or gap on this scorecard. If a proposed project doesn't move anything on the baseline, that's worth asking why.

**Update it on a cadence.** Quarterly is the minimum. Monthly if your product is moving fast or your market is volatile. The scorecard is only useful if it stays current.

**Share it openly.** The baseline scorecard should not live in a PM's private folder. It should be visible to engineering, design, leadership, and customer success. A shared understanding of where you actually stand is one of the most valuable things a PM can create.

---

## A Note on Honesty

The baseline scorecard only works if it's honest. A scorecard that makes the product look better than it is doesn't help anyone — it just delays the reckoning.

The goal is not to present a flattering picture. The goal is to present an accurate one, so that the improvements you make are pointed at real problems and measured against real numbers.
