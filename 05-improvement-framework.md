# 05 — Improvement Framework

**How to move from baseline to an evidence-backed roadmap.**

Once you have ground truth — a real product audit, real user research, a real competitive landscape, and a baseline scorecard — you're finally ready to talk about what to build next.

Not because you've run out of foundational work to do. Because now the work you do will be grounded in evidence instead of instinct.

---

## The Shift This Framework Creates

Without a baseline, a PM's roadmap is a list of bets. Some will pay off, most won't, and you'll rarely know why either way.

With a baseline, a PM's roadmap is a list of hypotheses — each tied to a specific metric you're trying to move, a specific user problem you're trying to solve, or a specific competitive gap you're trying to close. When you ship, you measure. When you measure, you learn. When you learn, your next bet is better informed.

That compounding effect is the difference between a PM who ships features and a PM who builds a better product.

---

## How to Prioritize from a Baseline

### Start with the biggest gap between where you are and where you need to be
Look at your baseline scorecard. What's the number that worries you most? That's usually where to start. Not because it's the most interesting problem — because it's the most important one.

### Map every candidate initiative to a baseline metric
Before any project makes it onto a roadmap, it should answer: which metric on our baseline scorecard does this move, and by how much? If the answer is unclear, that's a signal to either sharpen the hypothesis or reconsider the initiative.

### Sequence by impact and confidence
High impact + high confidence = do it now. High impact + low confidence = run a small experiment first. Low impact = deprioritize, regardless of how interesting it is.

### Protect space for user-driven improvements
Your user research will surface problems that don't show up cleanly in metrics. Make room for these. Some of the highest-leverage improvements come from fixing the friction that users have normalized — things they've stopped complaining about because they've given up expecting it to change.

---

## How to Measure Whether It Worked

Every initiative should have a defined success metric — pulled from the baseline — and a timeframe for measurement.

- **Before you ship**: document the baseline value of the metric you're targeting
- **After you ship**: measure the same metric in the same way
- **After enough time**: declare whether the hypothesis was confirmed, partially confirmed, or wrong — and document what you learned

This is not optional overhead. This is the only way to know if you're actually making the product better or just making it different.

---

## When to Revisit Ground Truth

The baseline scorecard is a living document, but the deeper foundational work — product audit, user research, competitive landscape — should be revisited when:

- You're about to make a significant strategic shift
- You've shipped a major change and the metrics moved in an unexpected direction
- A new competitor enters the market or an existing one makes a major move
- User sentiment shifts noticeably in your feedback channels
- You're onboarding a new PM or major stakeholder who needs context

Ground truth is not a one-time exercise. It's a practice.

---

## The Summary

| Foundation Work | What It Gives You |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Product Audit | An honest map of what you have |
| User Research | An honest picture of who uses it and why |
| Competitive Landscape | An honest read of where you stand in the market |
| Baseline Scorecard | A single source of truth to measure against |
| Improvement Framework | A way to move forward that you can actually measure |

Build on this foundation, and every decision you make is grounded in something real.
